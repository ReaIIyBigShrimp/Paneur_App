Intro

The leading monthly of Hungarian economics, Közgazdasági Szemle [Review of Economics] reported accurately the advances of the EEC’s enlargement (Great Britain, Ireland, Denmark), however, these articles stressed that Brussels had no concept how to cooperate with the Socialist countries. In the second half of the 1970s the perception of the Western integration was rather positive among economists: the encouraged inner mobility, the development of declining areas, the technological and structural adjustment was depicted as features of a successful adaptation through the deepening integration. Külgazdaság [Foreign Economy], the journal of the Institute of Prosperity and Market Research, presented several Western examples of effective economic adjustment. In the first years of the 1980s the Hungarian freedom of action and re-evaluation of the Western relations were the most significant topics in these journals.

In the columns of Külgazdaság the “optimal openness” of Hungary was tackled in 1981. Zoltán Krasznai and Gábor Oblath argued that Hungary was at the crossroads – it could open and catch up or else closing and drifting to periphery. In the following years the criticism of the ineffective Comecon cooperation escalated, the stagnation of the Eastern trade, the technological backwardness, and the lack of sources were more outspokenly pictured. Economists also were very sceptical of Gorbachev’s new initiatives to revitalize cooperation within Comecon and create an Eastern Common Market. According to András Köves, a single market could “come into being merely among market economies”.
